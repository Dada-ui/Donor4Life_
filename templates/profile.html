    FAQ ( Frequently Asked Questions )

    Organ donation is the process of surgically removing organs or tissues from one person (the donor) and transplanting them into another person (the recipient) who is in need of them.

    Organ donation can save lives and improve the quality of life for individuals suffering from organ failure or disease. It offers hope to patients awaiting transplants and their families.

    Organs that can be donated include the heart, lungs, liver, kidneys, pancreas, and intestines. Tissues that can be donated include corneas, skin, bone, heart valves, and blood vessels.

    Most people can be organ donors, regardless of age, race, or medical history. Donors can include individuals who have died from brain death or circulatory death, as well as living donors who can donate certain organs or tissues.

    Organ donation in most countries is altruistic, meaning donors do not receive payment for their organs. However, in some cases, living donors may receive reimbursement for expenses related to the donation process, such as medical costs and lost wages.

    Organ allocation is based on various factors, including medical urgency, compatibility between donor and recipient, waiting time, and the severity of the recipient's condition. Allocation policies are established by transplant organizations to ensure fair and equitable distribution of organs.

    Yes, individuals can change their organ donation decision at any time by updating their preferences in official registries, informing their family members, or specifying their wishes in advance directives.

    Individuals interested in becoming organ donors can register their decision with their country's official organ donor registry, indicate their wishes on their driver's license or identification card, and discuss their decision with family members.
